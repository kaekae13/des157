Miracle Modus

Miracle Modus by Seebs, LLC is an interactive app that displays beautiful, soothing patterns of light and sound. Though the app was initially intended for individuals with anxiety or autism, it has gained popularity with people that simply wish to destress and relax. One important thing to note, however, is that this app, by no means, is a cure for Autism. Autistic people commonly experience sensory overload and therefore, the simple and patterned visual imagery the app displays is meant to simply reduce sensory intensity. Many users have reportedly felt feelings of distress when viewing images where the patterning was unclear, but this distress fades as they come to understand the pattern.

The basic interface displays brightly-colored patterns and objects that responds to user activity (touching, dragging, gravity, etc.). Users can alter gears and navigate between different modes which give users options between different sounds, palettes, and lines. Different sound effects include ‘Metal Bell’ and ‘Airy Bell,’ while different palette choices include ‘Earth,’ ‘Ocean,’ and ‘Pastel.’ As for lines, there are 6 options that describe thickness and speed: ‘Thin,’ ‘Medium,’ ‘Thick,’ and ‘Fast,’ ‘Medium,’ and ‘Smooth,’ The last type of settings the app offers is scene settings which include options such as ‘Spiraling Shape (the arms drift towards the areas on the screen that have been touched by the user)’, ‘Painting Hexes (hexes drift across the screen, spreading and smearing different colors),’ and ‘Knights (squares move across the screen as they change color).’ These are just 3 of the 15 total different scene settings on the app. Each different scene setting is matched with a unique sound interaction. For each of these settings, there is an ‘enable’ and ‘disable’ function as well. The app is available for iOS 4.3 and is compatible with the iPhone, iPad, and iPod touch, as well as Android 2.2 and up.

The concept for the interface is strong. I believe visual imagery and ‘art’ can be used to help those with any sort of disability as well as mental health issue. In terms of interaction design, I think that the different types of user interactions are well thought out and that the selections of setting users are given allows for individual/creative and artistic freedom. The interface is lacking in terms of aesthetics, however. I believe the entire graphic identity of the app could be improved upon and made cleaner and more reflective of the purpose of the app I also believe the visuals on the interface could be improved to be more smooth in terms of movements and that more sound options could be added (sounds that may better produce relaxation). For example, certain types of music could be added to the app. The color schemes could also be improved upon and also, in my opinion, present another opportunity for user interaction (users could pick out colors for a unique palette which they could then save and use themselves). While reading reviews, I also discovered that some users experienced glitches or bugs and that, in rare cases, the visuals would be stuck on one scene setting - which led me to the conclusion that this web interface could be further developed and refined in terms of functionality and code/organization. I began to imagine the possibilities of this concept as a large-scale projection through which many people can interact with through movement sensors and microphones.

Swifty

Swifty is an app that was created by Apple. Essentially the app teaches people to code in a rather simple and concise language created by Apple, called Swift. The language was engineered by Apple’s compiler experts, making it safe to assume that it is compatible with existing languages/code as well as being relatively fast. The app offers lessons and its release was accompanied by the publishing of a free book that contains instructions as to how to use the language.

These tutorials/lessons are interactive: they guide users through different types of information, including the very basics of different data types as well as variables and functions, and users are encouraged to type out lines of code. Visually, the interface has been designed to look just like a coding environment though it functions more like an online quiz. Each tutorial begins with a brief lesson where a certain concept is explored and the previous lesson is reviewed. The tutorial then displays a block of code where some crucial piece of the code is missing and the user is expected to type out the correct value, name, etc. The code then ‘runs.’ The first 13 tutorials of the app are free, after which users must pay $2.99 to continue on with the lessons. The app is available only for iOS devices.

I believe that the concept of the app is compelling but that the visual aspect of the interface could be modified to make the app/the concept of coding less intimidating to novices. I also there are more opportunities for interaction not only with the interface but between users - incentives and rewards could be used to encourage users to advance through the lessons and perhaps users could even interact with one another. The app does well for providing a way to learn on the go and the overall graphic design of the interface is clean and well-organized. I believe that the app could have the potential to spur more ideas for different web interfaces that might also increase diversity in the engineering field and encourage more females to pursue coding in the future.
